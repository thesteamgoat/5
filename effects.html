Effects of Changes

Other factors impact the natural balance of ecosystems.  Both the biotic and the abiotic factors can create changes.  For example, how much or how little water is available can have different effects.  When there is a drought, which is a period of very little rainfall, organisms may perish or move away because of the lack of water.  Plants need water to grow and survive.  Less water means fewer plants will grow.  Animals that eat plants will have to find other plants somewhere else or they will die.  A flood is an overflow of water onto land that is normally dry.  Some organisms may thrive from the extra nutrients.  Others may perish or be forced to move away.

Sometimes new animals are brought into an area to graze.  This sometimes leads to overgrazing.  This practice uses up natural resources in that area.  With fewer plants to hold soil in place, the soil can blow away in the wind.  Animals that need the plants for food must find other areas to find food.  Overgrazing changes the habitat and makes it an unhealthy environment for the organisms that live there.

Highways cut off the natural movement, or migration, of animals.  Animals can be cut off from their food or water supply.  They can be killed by oncoming vehicles trying to reach what they need when they cross highways.  Highways also cover up land that was once a habitat for organisms.  The plants in the area can no longer grow, and the animals that lived there had to move somewhere else.
